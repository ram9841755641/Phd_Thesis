Use of Chimeric Human Immunodeficiency Virus Types 1 and 2 Reverse Transcriptases for Structure-Function Analysis and for Mapping Susceptibility to Nonnucleoside Inhibitors

Yang, Guozhe; Song, Qingbin; Charles, MacArthur; Drosopoulos, William C.; Arnold, Edward*; Prasad, Vinayaka R.

Free Access
Article Outline

Author Information

Department of Microbiology and Immunology, Albert Einstein College of Medicine, New York, New York; and *Center for Advanced Biotechnology and Medicine and Chemistry Department, Rutgers University, Piscataway, New Jersey, U.S.A.

Address correspondence and reprint requests to V. R. Prasad at Department of Microbiology and Immunology, Albert Einstein College of Medicine, 1300, Morris Park Avenue, Bronx, NY 10461, U.S.A.

Manuscript received June 8, 1995, accepted September 1, 1995.

Abstract

Summary: The human immunodeficiency virus type 1 and type 2 (HIV-1 and HIV-2) reverse transcriptases (RTs) are evolutionary related. To study the effect of homologous sequence replacements on polymerase function and to map the determinants of the lack of susceptibility of HIV-2 RT to nonnucleoside drugs, a series of chimeric HIV-1/HIV-2 RTs were constructed. Analysis of the chimeric RTs showed that wild-type levels of RNA-dependent DNA polymerase activity were retained when both finger and palm subdomains were exchanged as a unit between the two parental RTs. Analysis of enzymatically active chimeras for inhibition by the thiobenzimidazolone derivative TIBO R82150 showed that a segment of HIV-2 RT at 212-250, when placed in the HIV-1 RT context, conferred a 40-fold decrease in susceptibility to TIBO R82150. Site-directed mutagenesis of this segment found Tyr227 to be a key residue in this segment for the natural resistance of HIV-2 RT to TIBO R82150.

Drugs targeted to the human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) reverse transcriptase (RT) remain the only form of therapy for acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) in widespread use at present. It has been shown that HIV-1 RT is highly sensitive to a novel class of structurally dissimilar, nonnucleoside RT (NNRT) inhibitors, such as tetrahydro-5-methyl-6-(3-methyl-2-butenyl)imidazo-[4,5,1-jk][1,4]-benzodiazepin-2(1H)-thione (TIBO) (1,2), nevirapine (3), and others (4-8). The appearance of HIV-1 variants resistant to these and other anti-RT drugs is a major problem (9-12). Thus, a better understanding of the structure-function aspects of HIV RT is needed to help design drugs that are both efficacious and less likely to lead to resistance.

The HIV-1 RT is a heterodimer of 66 kDa (p66) and 51-kDa (p51) polypeptides. In the three-dimensional structure, the p66 resembles a right hand consisting of fingers, palm, thumb, connection, and RNase H subdomains. The p51, which is distal to the catalytic site, lacks the RNase H domain and is in a conformation that is different from that of p66 (13,14). HIV-2 RT is similar to HIV-1 RT in many ways. It is a heterodimer consisting of 68-kDa and 55-kDa polypeptides (15-17) and prefers magnesium as a divalent cation (15); the two RTs are 60% identical and 75% similar at the amino acid sequence level. The lack of susceptibility of HIV-2 RT to NNRT inhibitors suggests important structural differences. To gain structure-function insight and to map regions involved in drug susceptibility, we have created a series of chimeric HIV-1/HIV-2 RTs. Our studies with these chimeras suggest the importance of interactions between the finger and the palm subdomains for polymerase activity. Inhibition studies with active chimeras showed that, in addition to the known residues, Tyr181, Tyr188, Leu100, and Lys103, a segment spanning residues 212-250, specifically Tyr227, is important for the lack of susceptibility of HIV-2 RT to TIBO-R82150.

MATERIALS AND METHODS

Bacteria, RT Expression Plasmids

The E. coli strain HB101 was used for the expression of HIV RT. The construction of expression plasmids encoding trpE-fusions of HIV-1HXB2 (pHIV-1 RT, previously called pHRTRX2) (18,19) and HIV-2ROD RTs (pHIV-2 RT) (20) have been described earlier.

Introduction of Restriction Sites

Exchanges between HIV-1 and HIV-2 RTs were made by the use of five restriction sites, EcoRI, Xca I, Sty I, Pvu II, and Nhe I. Of these sites, EcoRI and Sty I were present only in wild-type HIV-2 RT and Xca I and Pvu II only in wild-type HIV-1 RT; the Nhe I site was absent from both. Each of these sites and combinations of sites, as required, were created in M13 derivatives of the two RTs (20) by oligonucleotide-directed mutagenesis (21). With the exception of the Nhe I site, creation of the restriction sites did not lead to undesirable amino acid substitutions. Introduction of the EcoRI site at codon 94 (Ile) in HIV-1 RT or of the Xca I site at codon 126 (Pro) of HIV-2 RT did not alter the amino acid sequence. Introduction of the Sty I site at codon 212 of HIV-1 RT (Trp212Leu) and of the Pvu II site at codon 250 of HIV-2 RT (Glu250Asp, Ile251Ser) led to substitutions that changed the amino acid residue to that present in the other parental RT. Finally, creation of the Nhe I site led to Asp324Gly and Ile326Ala alterations in HIV-1 RT and a Glu326Ala alteration in HIV-2 RT. The effect of each of these point mutations on the RT activity was minimal (data not shown). These restriction sites allow exchanges between or within the subdomains, as desired. The locations of the sites with respect to codon positions are shown in Fig. 1.

Construction of Chimeric RT Molecules

The chimeras were constructed with pHIV-1 RT and pHIV-2 RT as the parental plasmids and are designated to readily discern the arrangement of the HIV-1 and HIV-2 RT sequences in relation to the restriction site employed. For example, 1X2 refers to the chimera in which the HIV-1 sequence is at the 5' end and the HIV-2 sequence is at the 3' end, with the Xca I site marking the site of exchange. A common vector used for both the RT expression plasmids permitted the simple exchanges to be performed via the use of an a Hpa I site present 1.1 kb upstream of the RT sequences in both plasmids and one of the five restriction sites indicated. For example, to construct 1R2 and 2R1, a 1.48-kb Hpa I-EcoRI fragment of pHIV-1 RTR (HIV-1 RT construct containing the EcoRI site) and a 1.45-kb Hpa I-EcoRI fragment of pHIV-2 RT were exchanged and ligated (Fig. 1).

Chimeras with double crossovers were constructed using RT expression plasmids containing two of the selected restriction sites. Following double digestions, both the fragments were isolated from each parental plasmid. For example, in the construction of 1R2P1 and 2R1P2, 474-bp and 5,032-bp fragments were obtained by digesting pHIV-1 RTR with EcoRI and Pvu II. Digesting pHIV-2 RTX-P (HIV-2 RT in which the Pvu II and Xca I sites were introduced) with the same enzymes yielded a 5,109-bp fragment and the 474-bp fragment. The smaller fragments were exchanged in a ligation to yield 1R2P1 and 2R1P2 (Fig. 2). All chimeras were sequenced around the joints to ensure the absence of unintended mutations.

Site-directed Mutagenesis of Chimera 1S2P1

Mutagenesis of the 1S2P1 chimeric clone was via cassette mutagenesis (22). The base-paired region of the wide-type adaptor oligonucleotides, 5'-TCCATACCACTGGATGGGCTATGAACTATGGCCAACT-3' and 5'-ATTTAGTTGGCCATAGTTCATAGCCCATCCAGTGGTA-3', includes codons 227-237. Four mutant adaptors, designed to create the alterations Tyr227Phe, His228Leu, Tyr235His, and Thr237Asp, were ligated to the Bsp MI-digested 1S2P1 derivative, and the resultant clones were sequenced to confirm the presence of desired mutations and the absence of unintended mutations.

Back to Top | Article Outline
Induction of HIV RT and Preparation of Extracts

The wild-type HIV-1, HIV-2, and the chimeric RT plasmids were induced for expression by tryptophan starvation of bacteria carrying them and soluble lysates prepared as described previously (23). The protein concentration of all of the extracts, measured by the Bradford assay (24), was 1 g/l.

Back to Top | Article Outline
Determination of the RNA-dependent DNA Polymerase Activity

The RNA-dependent DNA polymerase (RDDP) activities were determined in a reaction buffer containing 50 mM Tris Cl at pH 8.0, 20 mM dithiothreitol, 60 mM NaCl, 0.05% Nonidet P-40, 10 g/ml poly (rC), 5 g/ml oligo (dG), 10 M [-32P] dGTP (specific activity, 1 Ci/mmol), and 10 mM MgCl2. The reactions, in a total volume of 50 l, were initiated by the addition of 1-5 l of the bacterial extract containing 100 ng of the wild-type HIV-1, HIV-2, or chimeric reverse transcriptase, as judged by immunoblot analysis of crude lysates alongside known quantities of purified HIV-1 RT heterodimer. The reactions were incubated for 60 min at 37C, and dGMP incorporation was determined as described previously (25).

Back to Top | Article Outline
Determination of Drug Sensitivity

The drug sensitivity of each chimera was determined by carrying out RDDP reactions, in duplicate, in the presence of increasing concentrations of the drug in the reaction cocktail described earlier. Reactions were carried out for 10 min, after which the levels of RDDP activity remaining at each drug concentration was determined. The activity of lysates induced with the pATH2 vector was subtracted from all values. The percentage RT activity remaining was plotted against the inhibitor concentration to determine 50% inhibitory concentration (IC50) values.

RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

Analysis of Chimeric RTs for RNA-dependent DNA Polymerase Activity

We first examined the expression plasmids encoding chimeric RTs for their ability to encode stable proteins. The crude lysate from induced bacteria was analyzed via sodium dodecyl sulfatepolyacrylamide gel electrophoresis to verify the accumulation of stable proteins. This analysis for the simple chimeras, shown in Fig. 3, indicated that all chimeras except 2X1 and 2N1 encode stable proteins (relevant bands indicated by arrowheads). A similar analysis of the six double crossover chimeras showed that all but one (IX2P1) encoded stable proteins (data not shown). Note that the trpE-fusions of HIV-1 and HIV-2 RT run differently, in agreement with previously reported electrophoretic mobility differences between the large polypeptides of native HIV-1 (66 kDa) and HIV-2 (68 kDa) RTs (16,17).

To measure the effect of piecing together evolutionarily related sequences on the function of the resultant hybrid RT molecules, crude lysates of all the chimeric RTs were tested for RNA-dependent DNA polymerase (RDDP) activity. Protein stabilities and the polymerase activities (presented as percentages of the most active parent, the HIV-2 RT) of simple chimeras are summarized in Fig. 1. Three chimeras showed significant levels of activity: 2S1, 2P1, and 1S2 (the chimera 1P2 was inactive). A common feature of these chimeras is that the finger and palm subdomains in each of them were derived from the same parental RT, with the exception of 2S1, in which one-third of the palm was derived from HIV-1 RT (even though the three-dimensional structure of HIV-2 RT is unknown, for clarity we refer to the HIV-2 RT sequences corresponding to various structural subdomains of HIV-1 RT as fingers, palm, thumb, and connection). On the other hand, the chimeras 2R1, 1R2, and 1X2 (the chimera 2X1 was unstable) with the fingers and palm made of different HIV-1 and HIV-2 RT sequences, encode stable but inactive or minimally active proteins. Surprisingly, the chimera 1N2, with the finger, palm, and thumb subdomains derived from a single parental RT, led to a stable but inactive protein (the chimera 2N1 was unstable) (Fig. 3).

Thus, as indicated by our results, the finger and the palm subdomains need to be derived from one parent for the hybrid molecule to properly fold into a catalytically active enzyme. This finding suggests that the interaction between the finger and the palm subdomains are more critical than the interaction of these subdomains with the rest of the molecule. In chimeric molecules made up of finger and palm subdomains derived from different parental RTs, such interactions are likely to be disrupted by the amino acid substitutions present within the homologous segment. Strikingly similar results were obtained by Shaharabany et al. (26).

Of the six chimeric genes that represent HIV-1 RT with internal segments of HIV-2 RT, all but one (1X2P1) encoded the synthesis of stable proteins (data not shown). With the exception of two (1S2P1 and 1R2X1), all displayed low levels of activity or no activity (Fig. 2). Interestingly, chimeric RTs 1S2P1 and 1R2X1, which contained replacements within HIV-1 RT of small stretches of 39 or 34 residues from HIV-2, displayed wild-type levels of activity. These results indicate that homologous sequences are better tolerated when the mixing of sequences is limited to small regions.

Mapping Decreased Susceptibility to a Nonnucleoside Inhibitor

To map regions of HIV-2 RT that determine decreased susceptibility to nonnucleoside inhibitors, enzymatically active chimeras and the two parental RTs were tested for sensitivity to inhibition by TIBO R82150. The IC50 values are presented in Fig. 4. As expected, HIV-1 RT was most sensitive (IC50: 1.0 M), while HIV-2 RT retained maximal activity at all drug concentrations tested, thus precluding the determination of the IC50 value. Five of the eight chimeras were poorly inhibited, similarly to their HIV-2 RT parent (2S1, 2P1, 1R2, 1R2S1, and 1R2P1). Examination of the primary structures of these chimeras (Figs. 2 and 4) showed that the HIV-2 RT sequences common to these hybrids span residues 93-211. Interestingly, this segment contains all the residues known to be altered in HIV-1 RT mutants resistant to TIBO R82150 or its derivatives: Leu100, Lys103, Glu138, Tyr181, and Tyr188 (11). It appears that the decreased susceptibility of hybrids 1R2S1 and 1R2P1 is predominantly due to the segment spanning residues 127-211, since a chimeric molecule consisting of residues 93-126 from HIV-2 RT (1R2X1) displayed only a tenfold increase in the IC50 value. The importance of the region 127-211 is highlighted by several observations: (a) the Tyr181Cys alteration in HIV-1 RT confers decreased susceptibility to a wide variety of NNRT compounds both at the level of viral replication (10,11,27) and at a biochemical level (28,29); (b) the replacement of residue 188 of HIV-1 with that from HIV-2 RT confers high-level decreased susceptibility to TIBO R82150 (30); (c) the replacement of residues 181 or 188 of HIV-1 RT with those from HIV-2 RT confers resistance to nevirapine, and the replacement of the HIV-2 RT segment between 181 and 188 with the corresponding segment from HIV-1 confers sensitivity to nevirapine (31); and (d) the alteration Glu138Lys is responsible for decreased susceptibility of an HIV-1 variant to inhibition in cell culture by TSAO ([2',5'-bis-O-(tertbutyldimethylsilyl)-3'-spiro-5-(4-amino-1, 2-oxathiole-2, 2-dioxide)]-(-D-pentofuranosyl derivatives) (11,32).

Thus, all the residues known to influence susceptibility to TIBO R82150 are located within the N-terminal 211 residues. Interestingly, however, a chimera (1S2) consisting of amino terminal 211 residues from HIV-1 RT (including residues 100, 103, 181, and 188 from HIV-1 RT) displayed an unexpected decrease in susceptibility (IC50: 40.0 M, 40-fold decrease). This is surprising since one would have expected 1S2 to retain the high sensitivity of the HIV-1 RT parent. This finding suggested that a determinant present downstream of residue 211 in HIV-2 RT plays a role in the decreased susceptibility of the 1S2 chimera. A second chimera (1S2P1) containing residues 212-250 of HIV-2 RT also showing a 40-fold decrease in susceptibility helped narrow the region responsible to the HIV-2 RT segment spanning residues 212-250. None of the mutations in HIV-1 RT shown to confer decreased susceptibility to TIBO compounds map to this region, although a Pro236Leu variant of HIV-1 with decreased susceptibility to BHAP in cell culture has been isolated (12).

Identification of Residues Critical for TIBO Susceptibility

A segment of HIV-2 RT downstream of residue 202 was also shown to play a role in susceptibility to TIBO compounds by other studies employing chimeric HIV-1/HIV-2 RT (33,34). However, neither of these studies mapped the determinants of decreased susceptibility to a specific subregion or residue. In order to identify residues in the HIV-2 RT segment responsible for decreased susceptibility to TIBO R82150, we wished to further investigate the chimera 1S2P1. The crystal structure of HIV-1 RT complexed with nevirapine shows that the drug binds in a hydrophobic pocket made up of five strands (13,35). The strands 6, 9, and 10 make up one side of the pocket, while the strands 12 and 13 form the other side. The chimera 1S2P1 contains HIV-2 RT sequences that correspond to 12 and 13 of HIV-1 RT. An inspection of the amino acid sequence showed that the segment of chimera 1S2P1 that is homologous to HIV-1 RT 12-13 differs from HIV-1 at four positions: 227, 228, 235, and 237. Therefore, it is likely that one or more of these residues contribute to the decreased susceptibility of 1S2P1. We surmised that the alteration of a key residue in the HIV-2 segment of 1S2P1 to that found in HIV-1 RT should revert the 1S2P1 chimera to an increased sensitivity, similar to the parental HIV-1 RT.

Each of the four residues in the HIV-2 segment of 1S2P1 were individually altered by mutagenesis to the corresponding HIV-1 residue: Tyr227Phe, His228Leu, Trp235His, and Thr237Asp. The mutant proteins were induced in bacteria, and their sensitivity to TIBO R82150 was determined as before. Our results (Table 1) show that of the four alterations, only Tyr227Phe lead to an increase in sensitivity from 40 M (for the parental 1S2P1) to 3 M (which is close to that of HIV-1 RT at 1 M). The alteration of residue 228 (His228Leu) led to minimal change in the IC50 value (35 M) relative to that of 1S2P1. These results indicate that the residue Tyr227 plays a role in the decreased susceptibility of 1S2P1 to TIBO. This finding is consistent with the detailed structure of the nevirapine-binding pocket of HIV-1 RT, which shows that the Phe227 of HIV-1 RT is within 3.8 ; of the drug (35). Interestingly, the alterations Trp235His and Thr237Asp both separately led to an unexpected decrease in susceptibility. The IC50 values were greater than that of 1S2P1 (TIBO IC50 values for both: >100.0 M). The fact that the alteration of HIV-2 RT residues Trp235 and Thr237 in the chimera 1S2P1 to those present in HIV-1 RT leads to a greater decrease in susceptibility than in the original chimera is intriguing. While we do not know the precise role of these residues in the decreased susceptibility of HIV-2 RT, the alteration of these residues appears to induce significant changes in the binding of the drug. Additionally, crystallographic data from HIV-1 RT (36) show that the residue 227 points toward the hydrophobic pocket, while residues 228 and 235 point away from the pocket. Therefore, the effect of altering residues 235 and 237 on susceptibility to TIBO is probably not due to the direct modulation of drug binding but rather may reflect an indirect effect on drug binding caused by the proximity of the altered residues to the binding pocket.

In order to investigate the role of HIV-2 residue Tyr227 in the TIBO sensitivity of HIV-1 RT, the alteration Phe227Tyr was introduced into the HIV-1 heterodimer expression construct pRT6H-PROT (25,37). The residues Leu228, His235, and Asp237 were also separately altered, for comparison, to the corresponding residues in HIV-2, and purified preparations of all four RT heterodimers were obtained. While all of the mutants formed stable heterodimers, the His235Trp mutant was enzymatically inactive. Our studies show that none of the alterations (Phe227Tyr, Leu228His, and Asp237Thr) affected the susceptibility of HIV-1 RT heterodimer to TIBO R82150 (data not shown). Thus, while the residue Tyr227 of HIV-2 RT does play an important role in the lack of susceptibility of HIV-2 RT to TIBO, the alteration of Phe227 to Tyr in HIV-1 RT is not sufficient to confer decreased susceptibility, probably owing to the fact that the Tyr227 in HIV-2 may require residues in its vicinity to exert its role in decreased susceptibility to TIBO.
