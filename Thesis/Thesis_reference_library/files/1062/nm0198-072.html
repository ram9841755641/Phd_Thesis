AMD3100, a small molecule inhibitor of HIV-1 entry via the CXCR4 co-receptor

George A. Donzella1, Dominique Schols2, Steven W. Lin3, José A. Esté2, Kirsten A. Nagashima4, Paul J. Maddon4, Graham P. Allaway4, Thomas P. Sakmar3, Geoffrey Henson5, Erik DeClercq2 & John P. Moore1

  1The Aaron Diamond AIDS Research Center, The Rockefeller University, 455 First Avenue, New York, New York 10016, USA

  2Rega Institute for Medical Research, Katholieke Universiteit, Minderbroederstraat 10, B-3000 Leuven, Belgium

  3Howard Hughes Medical Institute, The Rockefeller University, 1230 York Avenue, New York, New York 10021, USA

  4Progenics Pharmaceuticals, 777 Saw Mill River Road, Tarrytown, New York 10591, USA

  5AnorMED, No. 100-20353 64th Avenue, Langley, British Columbia, Canada V2Y 1N5

 Correspondence should be addressed to:J.P.M.;e-mail:jmoore@adarc.org

The bicyclam AMD3100 (formula weight 830) blocks HIV-1 entry and membrane fusion via the CXCR4 co-receptor, but not via CCR5. AMD3100 prevents monoclonal antibody 12G5 from binding to CXCR4, but has no effect on binding of monoclonal antibody 2D7 to CCR5. It also inhibits binding of the CXC-chemokine, SDF-1, to CXCR4 and subsequent signal transduction, but does not itself cause signaling and has no effect on RANTES signaling via CCR5. Thus, AMD3100 prevents CXCR4 functioning as both a HIV-1 co-receptor and a CXC-chemokine receptor. Development of small molecule inhibitors of HIV-1 entry is feasible.
